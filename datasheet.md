# Datasheet for Dataset
## Synthetic Longitudinal Multi-Omics Cohort for Incident Type 2 Diabetes Prediction

## Motivation

This dataset was created to support development and demonstration of an interpretable machine learning workflow for **incident type 2 diabetes prediction** using **longitudinal multi-omics**. The primary motivation was to provide a reproducible, benchmark for model design, exploratory analysis, calibration assessment, subgroup analysis, and feature prioritisation in the absence of a real accessible longitudinal multi-omic cohort.

The dataset is intended for methodological experimentation only. It was not designed to estimate real disease prevalence or real biomarker distributions for any specific population.

## Composition

### Dataset Type
- Fully synthetic tabular dataset
- Longitudinal extension with repeated measures
- Binary incident disease outcome with simulated time-to-event

### Number of Participants
- 3,500 simulated individuals

### Data Structure
The dataset consists of:

1. A **baseline table** containing one row per individual with:
   - demographics,
   - behaviours,
   - clinical biomarkers,
   - synthetic polygenic risk score,
   - transcript-like features,
   - protein-like features,
   - metabolite-like features,
   - simulated time-to-event,
   - incident T2D label.

2. A **longitudinal table** containing repeated observations at:
   - year 0,
   - year 2,
   - year 5.

### Feature Groups

#### Demographic and Behavioural
- age
- sex
- simulated ancestry stratum
- smoking
- physical activity
- family history

#### Clinical
- body mass index
- HbA1c
- fasting glucose
- HDL cholesterol
- triglycerides
- systolic blood pressure

#### Genetic
- synthetic polygenic risk score

#### Transcript-like Features
- 18 simulated transcript-style variables

#### Proteomic Features
- 16 simulated protein-style variables, including:
  - adiponectin-like signal,
  - SHBG-like signal,
  - ACY1-like signal,
  - GFRA1-like signal,
  - inflammatory and metabolic module proxies.

#### Metabolomic Features
- 24 simulated metabolite-style variables, including:
  - branched-chain amino acids,
  - aromatic amino acids,
  - GlycA-like inflammation,
  - lactate and related metabolites,
  - lipid-related features.

#### Longitudinal Summaries
Derived during analysis:
- baseline values
- per-year slopes
- trajectory clusters
- unsupervised omics subtype assignments
- predicted risk deciles

## Collection Process

No real-world collection took place. The dataset was generated by simulation.

### Simulation Logic
The dataset was generated by:
- sampling baseline demographic and behavioural variables,
- constructing latent biological axes for insulin resistance, inflammation, and beta-cell stress,
- generating observed biomarkers and omics from those latent axes,
- simulating repeated measurements over time,
- simulating incident disease risk using a biologically informed synthetic rule,
- adding modest missingness to mimic incomplete observation.

### External Data Sources
No raw external datasets were used.

The simulation was conceptually inspired by patterns described in the biomedical literature on:
- incident T2D prediction,
- branched-chain amino acid burden,
- inflammatory biomarkers,
- circulating proteomic risk markers,
- longitudinal multi-omics in prediabetes,
- modest additive value of polygenic risk.

These literature patterns informed only qualitative directionality, not direct numerical reproduction.

## Preprocessing and Cleaning

### Baseline Handling
- Mild missingness was artificially introduced into selected variables.
- Median imputation was used in the predictive pipeline.
- Standard scaling was applied to continuous predictors before penalised regression.

### Longitudinal Handling
- Longitudinal trajectories were summarised into slope-based features.
- Hierarchical clustering was applied to trajectory summaries.
- Principal component analysis was applied to the omics matrix after imputation and scaling.

### Outcome
- The binary endpoint is simulated incident type 2 diabetes.
- Time-to-event and censoring were also simulated to support temporal risk visualisation.

## Uses

### Recommended Uses
- prototyping disease-prediction pipelines,
- testing notebook workflows,
- teaching interpretable machine learning,
- benchmarking calibration and subgroup analysis,
- generating figures and manuscript structure for synthetic proof-of-concept work.

### Non-Recommended Uses
- training a clinically deployable diabetes predictor,
- benchmarking real-world fairness,
- estimating true biomarker distributions,
- estimating ancestry effects,
- deriving biological conclusions about human disease.

## Distribution

This dataset is distributed as part of the repository and exists only in synthetic form. It does not contain protected health information, patient records, or identifiable personal data.

## Biases and Limitations

Although synthetic datasets avoid privacy concerns, they can still encode design bias.

Important limitations include:

- The data reflect assumptions chosen by the author.
- Correlation structure is artificial and simplified.
- The ancestry strata are placeholders for robustness testing only.
- Biological complexity is reduced to a few latent axes.
- Clinical care effects such as treatment, medication change, adherence, and drop-out are not explicitly modelled.
- The simulated endpoint is not equivalent to adjudicated real-world diagnosis.

Because of these limitations, the dataset should be treated as a **benchmarking scaffold**, not a surrogate for real cohort science.

## Ethical and Legal Considerations

This dataset contains no real human data. Therefore:
- no participant consent was required,
- no personal data are present,
- no identifiable records are included.

However, users should avoid over-interpreting subgroup differences or presenting synthetic outputs as evidence about real populations.

## Relationship to Models

This dataset was used to train and evaluate:
- clinical-only logistic models,
- clinical plus polygenic risk models,
- clinical plus metabolomic models,
- clinical plus proteomic models,
- combined multi-omics models,
- multi-omics plus trajectory models.

The derived model outputs include:
- cross-validated predicted probabilities,
- calibration summaries,
- subgroup performance tables,
- feature stability rankings,
- risk-decile analyses.

## Summary

This datasheet describes a synthetic longitudinal multi-omics benchmark for incident type 2 diabetes prediction. Its value lies in supporting transparent pipeline development, interpretability testing, and reproducible computational demonstration. Its limitations lie in the fact that it is not real biology, not real epidemiology, and not suitable for clinical use without full redevelopment and validation on real data.
